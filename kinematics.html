Kinematics

Three-dimensional space

Given a cartesian coordinate system, a point in three-dimensional space can be represented by a vector

r⇀ = x·x^ + y·y^ + z·z^ = x y z ∈ ℝ 3

where x, y and z denote the coordinates of this point and x^, y^ and z^ are orthonormal basis vectors.

A vector v⇀ = ( vx , vy , vz ) can be multiplied by a scalars and added to other vectors, so that linear combinations can be constructed

w⇀ = α · v⇀ + β · u⇀ = α · vx + β · ux α · vy + β · uy α · vz + β · uz ∈ ℝ 3 , α , β ∈ ℝ , v⇀ , u⇀ ∈ ℝ 3

Vectors can also be multiplied by the dot product and the cross product

v⇀ · u⇀ = vx vy vz · ux uy uz = vx · ux + vy · uy + vz · uz ∈ ℝ
v⇀ × u⇀ = vx vy vz × ux uy uz = vy uz - vz uy vz ux - vx uz vx uy - vy ux ∈ ℝ 3

The distance dAB between two points A and B in space results from the absolute value of the difference vector

dAB = | rB⇀ - rA⇀ | = ( rB⇀ - rA⇀ ) · ( rB⇀ - rA⇀ ) = ( xB - xA ) 2 + ( yB - yA ) 2 + ( zB - zA ) 2

This is the Pythagorean Theorem in three dimensions.

Trajectory of particle

The position r⇀ of a moving particle changes with the time t. Therefore, its trajectory is described by a vector-valued function:

t ∈ ℝ ↦ r⇀ (t) = x(t) y(t) z(t) ∈ ℝ 3

where the coordinates x(t), y(t) and z(t) are functions of the time t.

The set of all visited points { r⇀ (t) | t ∈ ℝ } describes a certain curve in space.

In case of periodic motion there is time period T, such that r⇀ (t+T) = r⇀ (t) for all times t.

Uniform motion

A simple example for an non-trivial trajectory is the uniform motion with constant velocity:

r⇀ (t) = r⇀0 + v⇀ · t = x0 y0 z0 + vx vy vz · t = x0 + vx · t y0 + vy · t z0 + vz · t

where r⇀0 = r⇀(0) is the intial position of the particle at the time t=0 and v⇀ is the velocity at which it moves.

The curve of this trajectory is a straight line.

Velocity

The velocity of a particle is the rate of change of its position.

For a certain time interval [ t1 , t2 ] the average velocity ist defined by the difference quotient:

< v⇀ > = Δ r⇀ Δ t := r⇀ (t2) - r⇀ (t1) t2 - t1 = 1 t2 - t1 x(t2) - x(t1) y(t2) - y(t1) z(t2) - z(t1)

The absolute value < v > = < v⇀ > · < v⇀ > is the average speed of the particle.

The instantaneous velocity of a particle at a certain time t results in the limit Δt = t2 - t1 → 0 so that it is given by the time derivative of the position vector:

v⇀ (t) = vx(t) vy(t) vz(t) := lim Δt→0 Δ r⇀ Δ t = r⇀ • (t) = d r⇀ (t) d t = x•(t) y•(t) z•(t) = dx(t) dt dy(t) dt dz(t) dt ∈ ℝ 3

The absolute value v=|v⇀|= v⇀·v⇀ of the velocity is the instantaneous speed of the particle.

The velocity vector v⇀= v·t^ is tangential to the trajectory and points towards the movement direction.

Only in for uniform motion both the average and instantaneous velocity are the same.

Acceleration

The acceleration of a particle is the rate of change of its velocity. It is defined by the time derivative of the velocity:

a⇀ (t) = ax(t) ay(t) az(t) := d v⇀ (t) d t = v•x(t) v•y(t) v•z(t) = dx(t) dt dy(t) dt dz(t) dt ∈ ℝ 3

The acceleration is composed of two components, with are tangent and perpendicular to the trajectory:

a⇀ (t) = d dt ( v·t^ ) = dv dt ·t^ + v· dt^ dt = dv dt ·t^ + v2 R ·n^

where n^ is the normal unit vector and R denotes the radius of curvature of the trajectory.

Uniform acceleration

In this case the acceleration is time-independent. Velocity and position can be derived by time integration:

a⇀ (t) = a⇀0 = const
v⇀ (t) = ∫0t a⇀ (t) dt + v⇀0 = a⇀0 · t + v⇀0
r⇀ (t) = ∫0t v⇀ (t) dt + r⇀0 = 12 a⇀0 · t2 + v⇀0 · t + r⇀0

where v⇀0 and r⇀0 are constants of integration that describe the intial velocity and position of the particle.

The motion takes place in a plane that is spanned by the vectors v⇀0 and a⇀0 .

The motion is composed of a linearly accelerated motion and a uniform motion, so that the combination results in a parabolic trajectory.

Uniform circular motion

The particle moves inside the xy-plane on a circle centered at the origin with radius R.

In polar coordinates, only the angle φ=ωt+φ0 changes at a constant angular velocity ω:

r⇀ (t) = R cos(ωt+φ0) sin(ωt+φ0)
v⇀ (t) = ω R -sin(ωt+φ0) cos(ωt+φ0)
a⇀ (t) = - ω2 R cos(ωt+φ0) sin(ωt+φ0) = - ω2 r⇀ (t)

The motion is periodic with a period T=2π/ω so that r⇀ (t+T) = r⇀ (t)

Because the acceleration is perpendicular to the direction of motion, only the direction of movement changes, not the speed.

The absolute values of the position r=R, velocity v=ωR and acceleration a=ω2R are constants.

Problems

River crossing

A ferry commutes between two points A and B on different sides of a river.

The river has a width of 400 meters and the point B is located 400 meters downstream from the point A.

The river has a flow rate of 2 meters per second. The boat cruises at a speed of 5 meters per second relative to the water.

How long does the ferry take to travel between the two points?

Range of artillery

A medieval cannon shoots cannon balls at a velocity of 200 meters per second.

What is the maximum range of the cannon when firing at the optimum firing angle?

Gravity acceleration is 10 meters per second.
